People in Swansea and Cardiff are still breathing air with the most tiny particle pollution in it, according to analysis of PM2.5 levels in 2022.

It also found that the months of March and January had particularly high levels of PM2.5 last year.

In South Wales PM 2.5 levels are still more than twice as high as the World Health Organization’s (WHO) guidelines, although they are within the limits allowed by the UK and Welsh government.

PM 2.5 is considered dangerous because it gets into the entire body and affects blood and respiratory systems. The WHO considers it a carcinogen.

The worst PM2.5 average reading in south Wales last year was measured in Cardiff, where the hourly average over the year was 10.55 micrograms of PM2.5 per cubic metre [AURN].

In Swansea one location measured an average of 10.37 micrograms of PM2.5 per cubic metre [AURN Swansea roadside]. But ANOTHER [locally managed] Swansea site recorded an average of 11.27 micrograms of PM2.5 per cubic metre. Other areas that are monitored include Port Talbot and Newport and had less than 10micrograms etc.

The WHO recommend keeping annual PM 2.5 levels at 5 µg/m3 and it say this should be reflected in legislation and policy.

Wales has maintained a 20µg/m3 per year target in line with UK and EU regulations. However, in March last year the UK government suggested a new target for 2040, of 10µg/m3, a target that Wales does not yet meet.

In Cardiff, the annual PM 2.5 particulate matter levels measured every hour were low as 8.52 in 2021 during the Covid-19 lockdown.[AURN] Prior to coronavirus pandemic, Cardiff's PM 2.5 levels were 10.09 in 2018 and 11.24 in 2019.[AURN]

When analysed by month, South Wales has higher concentrations of PM 2.5 pollution in the spring and winter months and lower concentrations in the summer and early autumn.

While this varies slightly across the region, broadly speaking PM 2.5 levels are highest in March, December and January, and lowest in July and September.





Professor Frank Kelly, who leads an Environmental Research Group at Imperial College London, points to the spraying of pesticides and fertilisers as a reason for the increase in fine particulate matter in spring. “At this time there is a lot of secondary PM2.5 being generated by action of our continental neighbours like France and the Netherlands as farmers are spraying their field with pesticides and fertilizers at this time”.

He says that these particles "end up as precursors for secondary PM 2.5 which is blown into the UK."

Although south Wales has cleaner air than the cities of India or China, the WHO warns of a clear link between air pollution and deaths caused by circulatory, respiratory and lung cancer deaths. The figures for South Wales remain nearly twice as high as the WHO's safe levels.

PM 2.5 is fine inhalable particle with diameters that are generally 2.5 micrometers and smaller.

In fact, it's hard for us to understand how small 2.5 micrometres is with numbers and symbols.

Let's imagine a single hair from your hair.

We'll start by plucking a strand of hair - you can use your own, or borrow one from a friend with a full head of hair.

The average human hair is about 70 micrometres in diameter.

If I take the 1 hair I just plucked and split it back into 30 strands, I can estimate 1 PM 2.5 particle.

This size is particularly dangerous because such miniscule particles can penetrate human lungs with ease and the tiniest particles can even enter the bloodstream.

Let's imagine one more thing. Please draw a cubic box in your head.

People put a single PM 2.5 particle into a cubic metre box that is one metre across, one metre long and one metre high, and express it again in numbers and symbols (µg/m3 ).

µg/m3 is the symbol we use to measure the concentration of an air pollutant. It is a measure of concentration in terms of mass per unit volume.

1 µg/m3 means that there is one particle of PM 2.5 in a box of air that is 1 m long, 1 m wide, and 1 m high.

10 µg/m3 for PM 2.5 means that 10 particles of PM 2.5 size or smaller were measured inside an air box of the same size.

Particles are likely to enter through the respiratory tract and bloodstream of citizens who enjoy outdoor activities or ordinary people who open windows and ventilate indoors.

Air quality campaigners have said the Welsh government is missing a golden opportunity to clean up the country’s air with a new law that will come into force in September. The Welsh government has just released a white paper on the proposed act, but campaigners say specific targets are needed to lower the levels of Nitrogen Dioxide gas and of tiny particles (known as PM2.5) that people breathe in many of the country’s built-up areas.

Despite the white paper stating, “The strongest body of evidence connecting an air pollutant with mortality and other morbidity effects involves fine particulate (PM2.5),” it could be three years of “consultation” before setting a five-year target for PM2.5 pollution. It is for this reason that the hackles of groups such as Healthy Air Cymru and CEIH have been raised.

Ross Matthewman, Head of Policy, and Campaigns for Chartered Institute of Environmental Health (CEIH) said, “The Welsh Government have missed a golden opportunity to introduce ambitious, robust environmental protection targets with this Bill. There was an opportunity to lay down a marker by introducing robust targets for both PM2.5 and NO2 in line with WHO guidelines, yet what has emerged has been rather underwhelming.”

Those sentiments were echoed by Healthy Air Cymru, an umbrella group of organisations in Wales working to improve Welsh air quality. In a consultation reply to the proposed Bill, HAC also noted that the proposed act did not include any specific targets for either NO2 or PM2.5. Healthy Air Cymru wants to enshrine the World Health Organisation targets into the Act. The WHO has rapidly reduced its PM2.5 guidelines in recent years, as more research highlights the particles danger to public health in any concentrations. In 2021, the WHO reduced its guidance to a daily mean of 5.0 µg/m3.

Wales uses Westminster’s PM2.5 annual mean target of 20.0 µg/m3, but Asthma + Lung UK Cymru says under that under current WHO guidance at 5.0 µg/m3, every care home, school, hospital, and GP in Wales would be in an area where PM2.5 is above the WHO guideline.

The fractured landscape of air quality monitoring in Wales.

The government intends to use the local authority air quality measuring infrastructure to measure air quality, with no further financial resources to be committed. That monitoring infrastructure is fragmented with Swansea and Cardiff measuring a full spectrum of pollutants for example, while Merthyr Tydfil, which counts Britain’s only open-cast coal mine among its residents, measures only NO2.

Ciaran Donaghy, a policy advisor at CEIH thinks there hasn’t been enough money given to local authorities to monitor air quality. “Underpinning much of all of this, is the issue of local authority resource capacity and funding, which has faced considerable cuts over a sustained period”. Unless local authorities have the money, he says, “any monitoring or enforcement will be hamstrung”.

Burning coal & wood at home

Almost a third of Wales’ PM2.5 pollution comes from people burning wood or coal at home and the new law will give councils’ greater abilities to fine houses where smoke is coming from a chimney in a smoke control area. At present, unauthorised smoke emissions from chimneys in a smoke control area is a criminal offence. This requires physical entry to the offending homes which is problematic for local authorities. By making this a civil offence, council enforcement officers merely need to stand outside the offending house and witness smoke coming from the chimney to issue fines. On paper this is welcome news (for the councils), but once again, the Welsh government delegates both the declaration of smoke control areas and enforcement actions to local councils.

Wales is Britain’s PM2.5 hotspot

Wales already lags the rest of the UK with PM2.5 emissions. Emissions of PM2.5 in Wales were estimated to be 6,000 tonnes in 2020, a 30% decline since 2005. But Wales still emits nearly twice as much PM2.5 per head of population, as the rest of the United Kingdom. With just 4.60% of the UK population, Wales emits 8.0% of the UK’s total PM2.5 emissions.

A simpler solution to enduring another three years of “consultation”, reducing PM 2.5 emissions in Wales by 30.0% instantly at no cost, and reducing the burden on local authorities, could simply be an immediate ban of domestic wood and coal burners in urban areas of Wales. A recent University of Sheffield study showed that even very “efficient” modern wood burners had little impact on elevated PM2.5 levels in houses.
